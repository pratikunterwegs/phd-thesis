\ctparttext{
    The GPS-tracking revolution announced a new era of rich animal positioning datasets in the late 2000s and through the 2010s.
    Yet smaller animals, and especially small birds, remained beyond the reach of GPS-tracking, as these tags were, and in many cases remain, too heavy for them to carry.
    % Aquatic species were also poorly served, as GPS tags are not suitable for underwater tracking.\\
    
    \medskip
    
    Recent collaborations between animal ecologists and computer scientists has led to the development and adoption of a host of new lightweight, `high-throughput' tracking technologies.
    In this part, I cover two efforts: first, and as an introduction, a review of what high-throughput animal tracking has revealed, and what more it promises.
    Second, my own work on pre-processing the vast datasets that high-throughput tracking generates, to make them suitable for ecological inference.

    \medskip

    This part lays out the landscape of current research and methods in animal movement ecology, and it is on this landscape that later chapters are located.
}
